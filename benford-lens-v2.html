The Science

How it works

A step-by-step breakdown of the mathematics behind the detection.

1
Load & convert to grayscale
The image is loaded in-browser and converted to grayscale (luminance channel). It's resized to a maximum of 800px to keep analysis fast, then cropped to a multiple of 8 pixels so it divides cleanly into blocks.
2
8×8 DCT block decomposition
The image is divided into 8×8 pixel blocks — exactly as JPEG compression does — and a 2D Discrete Cosine Transform is applied to each block. This converts pixel brightness values into frequency coefficients, revealing how much energy exists at different spatial frequencies.
3
Extract AC coefficients
From each 8×8 transformed block, we discard the DC coefficient (the average brightness) and keep the 63 AC coefficients — these capture texture, edges, and structure. A typical image produces hundreds of thousands of AC coefficients.
4
Tally leading digits
We take the absolute value of each coefficient ≥ 1.0 and extract its first digit (1–9). These are tallied into a distribution histogram. Real photographs produce a distribution that closely follows Benford's Law.
5
Measure deviation & score
Eight statistical measures — chi-squared deviation, KL divergence, mean absolute deviation, KS statistic, kurtosis, skewness, digit-1 excess ratio, and Pearson correlation — quantify how much the observed distribution deviates from the theoretical Benford curve. These combine into a final synthetic probability score.

The Creator

About this project

Benford Lens grew out of a question: can you detect AI-generated images without training a neural network on millions of examples?

The answer turns out to be yes — using a mathematical principle first observed in 1881 by Simon Newcomb while flipping through logarithm tables. Real photographs, captured from the physical world through a lens, carry the statistical fingerprint of nature in their frequency domain. AI-generated images, synthesised pixel by pixel, often don't.

This tool applies Benford's Law to DCT frequency coefficients — the same frequency representation JPEG compression uses. By measuring how much an image's coefficient distribution deviates from the expected Benford curve, we can estimate the probability that it was artificially generated.

The academic foundation is the 2020 paper "On the use of Benford's Law to detect GAN-generated images" by Bonettini et al. at Politecnico di Milano, which demonstrated >99% accuracy on benchmark datasets. This implementation extends their approach with eight statistical metrics, adaptive sharpening for blurry images, and an interpretable per-digit breakdown.

Everything runs in your browser. No images are stored. No data is sent anywhere. The code is transparent about what it measures and why — because forensic tools should show their working.
